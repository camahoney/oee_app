Hydraulics
High Pressure Safety

Session Overview

Duration: 30-45 Minutes (Workshop Mode)

Goal: To instill a deep respect for high-pressure fluid power. Operators must understand that hydraulic fluid is not just "messy oil"—it is a lethal energy source capable of silent, invisible amputation.

Preparation Checklist
  • [ ] Projector/TV for Slide Deck.
  • [ ] Prop: Clean piece of cardboard attached to a stick.
  • [ ] Prop: Sample of high-pressure hose (cutaway if possible).
  • [ ] PPE: Safety Glasses, Leather Gloves.
Core Concepts (The Big 3)

1. Pressure is Force: Pascal's Law means tiny pumps lift massive loads.

2. The Pinhole Leak: The most dangerous hazard. Invisible, high-velocity stream.

3. Stored Energy: Accumulators hold pressure even after LOTO.

Instructor Notes

"Hydraulics is the most underestimated hazard in the plant. People respect electricity because it shocks. They respect robots because they move. They treat hydraulics like 'plumbing'. It is NOT plumbing."

Module 1: Physics of Pain
Slide 3: Pascal's Law

Explain: Use the diagram. "Force equals Pressure times Area."

Analogy: A hydraulic press is a lever. We trade distance for force. The pump moves a lot of fluid (distance) to move the cylinder a tiny bit (force).

Ask: "Why can't I just hold my thumb over the leak to stop it?"

Answer: "Because 3,000 PSI is 3,000 pounds on every square inch. Your thumb is about 1 square inch. Imagine parking a Toyota Camry on your thumb. That is the force."

Slide 5: Injection Injuries
WARNING:

This is the most critical part of the lesson. You must scare them straight.

The Mechanism: A pinhole leak creates a stream thinner than a hair. It pierces the skin instantly. It doesn't hurt much at first—it feels like a bee sting or a wire prick.

The Aftermath: The oil spreads down the tendon sheaths in the hand. The human body reacts to the oil by swelling. The swelling cuts off blood flow (Compartment Syndrome). The tissue dies (Gangrene).

The Treatment: It requires immediate surgery to slice the hand open and scrape out the oil. If you wait 6 hours ("I'll walk it off"), you lose the finger. If you wait 24 hours, you lose the hand.

Module 2: Practical Skills
Slide 7: The Cardboard Test

Demonstrate: Hold the cardboard on a stick.

Technique: Run the cardboard along the hose at a distance of 3-4 inches.

Result: If there is a leak, the cardboard will get wet or be cut. Your hand stays safe.

Slide 8: Stored Energy (Bleed Down)

The Trap: You Lockout the electrical panel. The motor stops. Is the machine safe?

NO. The accumulator is a pressurized nitrogen tank pushing on the oil. The lines are still full of 3,000 PSI.

The Fix: You must cycle the valve levers (forward/back) after the pump is off to release the trapped pressure back to the tank. Watch the pressure gauge drop to zero.

Assessment

Competency Check

Instructor: Verify each trainee can answer these correctly.

Q1: What should you do if you suspect a hose is leaking?

A: Use cardboard to find it. Never use hands.

Q2: You feel a tiny prick on your finger from oil. It doesn't hurt much. What do you do?

A: Report to ER immediately. State "High Pressure Injection".

Q3: Why isn't Electrical Lockout enough for hydraulics?

A: Stored energy (Accumulators/Gravity) keeps pressure in the lines.
