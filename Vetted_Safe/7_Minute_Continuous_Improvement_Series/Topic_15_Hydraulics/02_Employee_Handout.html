1. The Invisible Enemy: Injection

Figure 1: Fluid penetrating deep tissue.

What happens?

Hydraulic fluid is toxic. When injected under pressure, your body reacts violently.

  • 0 Hours: Tiny prick. Numbness.
  • 4 Hours: Throbbing pain. Swelling.
  • 12 Hours: Extreme necrosis (tissue death).
  • Result: Amputation if not treated by a hand surgeon instantly.
EMERGENCY ACTION:
If you get injected, do not wash it. Do not bandage it. Go to the ER. Show them this paper: "I have a High-Pressure Injection Injury from Hydraulic Fluid."

2. Finding Leaks (The Cardboard Method)

Never run your hand along a hose. Use a target.

  1. Get a stiff piece of cardboard or wood.
  2. Wear safety glasses and full face shield.
  3. Move the cardboard along the hose.
  4. Check the cardboard for wet spots.

Figure 2: Correct Leak Detection.

3. Zero Energy Verification

Before breaking a line (changing a hose/valve):

Step 1: Lockout Electrical Pump Motor.
Step 2: Wait for motion to stop (Cylinders settle).
Step 3: Bleed Accumulator (Cycle valves or open bleed valve).
Step 4: Verify Pressure Gauge reads "0".
Step 5: Crack fitting slowly (Cover with rag to catch spray).

5. Quick Reference

Hoses

Check for: Blisters, cracked rubber cover, rubbing against metal.

Fittings

Check for: Wetness, thread exposure. Never tighten under pressure.

Oil

Check: Level (Sight glass), Temp (Below 140F), Color (Clear/Amber, not Milky).
