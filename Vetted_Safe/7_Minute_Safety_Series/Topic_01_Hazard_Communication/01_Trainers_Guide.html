HazCom
Chemical Safety

1. Value Proposition & Goals

Goal: Reduce chemical injuries (burns, inhalation) by ensuring every worker understands the "Right to Know": What is here, what it does, and how to stay safe.

Why it matters: A good HazCom program prevents "blindness". If you can't read the label, you are guessing clearly.

Preparation Checklist
  • [ ] Projector/TV for Slide Deck.
  • [ ] Prop: Real chemical containers from the plant (Good & Bad examples).
  • [ ] Prop: The Physical SDS Binder (or Tablet).
  • [ ] Drill: "SDS Scavenger Hunt" cards.
2. Core Regulatory Elements (OSHA)

Written Program: We must have a written plan describing how we handle chemicals. (Show the plan).

Chemical Inventory: A master list of all hazardous substances on site.

Labels & SDS: The GHS Standard guarantees a consistent format worldwide.

Trainer Emphasis

"Tie this to recent history. Mention specific splash incidents or inhalation complaints. Make it real."

GHS: Globally Harmonized System. "The Diamond tells the story."

Module 1: Labels
3. Decoding the Label

Every GHS label must have:

  • Product Identifier: What is it? (e.g., Acetone).
  • Signal Word:
    • DANGER = Severe Hazard.
    • WARNING = Moderate Hazard.
  • Pictograms: Visual Symbols.
Activity: Pictogram Review

Show the "GHS Chart" slide. Ask group to identify common hazards:

  • Flame: Flammables (Gas, Solvents).
  • Corrosion: Acids, Caustics (Eye/Skin damage).
  • Skull & Crossbones: Acute Toxicity (Pesticides).
  • Health Star: Chronic/Long-term (Lead, Carcinogens).
6. Safe Handling & Storage

Secondary Containers: The #1 Violation. If you pour it into a spray bottle or jug, you MUST label it immediately. "If you can't label it, toss it."

Storage Rules: Incompatibles (Acids vs Bases) must be separated. Keep containers closed.

Module 2: SDS
4. Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

The SDS is your manual. It has 16 standard sections. Focus on the "Big 5" for line employees:

  1. Sec 1: Identification (What is it?).
  2. Sec 2: Hazards (Warning/Danger).
  3. Sec 4: First Aid (Medical response).
  4. Sec 6: Accidental Release (Spill response).
  5. Sec 8: PPE (Gloves, Respirators).
Drill: The 2-Minute Lookup

"I want everyone to find the SDS for [Common Chemical]. Find Section 4. What do you do if you get it in your eyes?"

(Time them. If it takes >2 mins, our system is broken).

5. Routes of Exposure & Controls

Routes: Inhalation (breathing), Absorption (skin), Ingestion (eating), Injection (puncture).

Controls (Hierarchy):

  • Engineering: Fume hoods, ventilation.
  • Admin: Procedures, no food in chemical areas.
  • PPE: The last line of defense (Nitrile gloves vs Latex).
7. Emergency Response

Line Employee Role: Recognize -> Retreat -> Report.

Do NOT clean up a spill unless you are trained and have the kit. Activate the alarm for major releases.

Employee Rights

Employees have the right to access SDSs and exposure records without fear of retaliation.

8. Competency Check

Q1: What does the "Exclamation Mark" pictogram mean?

A: Irritant (skin/eye) or Narcotic effects.

Q2: Where do we go to find an SDS?

A: [Site Specific Location - e.g. Yellow Binder in Breakroom].

Q3: You find an unlabeled bottle of clear liquid. What do you do?

A: Do not use it. Report it. Get it disposed of or identified.
