Ladders
Fall Prevention

1. Value Proposition & Goals

Goal: Stop the fall before it happens. 80% of falls are due to bad setup.

The Stat: Ladders account for 81 workplace fatalities every year. Gravity does not forgive mistakes.

Preparation Checklist

2. The "Silent Killer": Wrong Ratings

Using a "Household" ladder on a job site is asking for a collapse.

  • Type IA: 300 lbs (Heavy Duty/Industrial).
  • Type I: 250 lbs.
  • Type II: 225 lbs (Medium Duty).
  • Type III: 200 lbs (Household - DO NOT USE).

Trainer Emphasis

"It's YOUR weight PLUS your tools. If you are 200lbs and carry a 40lb box, a Type III ladder might snap."

Module 1: Physics
3. The 4:1 Rule (Extension Ladders)

For every 4 feet up, the base must be 1 foot out. If it's too steep, it tips back. Too shallow, the feet slide out.

3 Points of Contact

Always maintain 3 points touching the ladder.

  • 2 Feet + 1 Hand (Climbing).
  • 2 Hands + 1 Foot (Positioning).

NEVER carry a box up with both hands. Use a rope or belt.

4. The "Belt Buckle" Rule

Keep your belt buckle between the two side rails.

If your buckle crosses the rail, you are OVERREACHING. This is the #1 cause of tip-overs.

Module 2: Scenarios
5. Go / No-Go Drills
Scenario A: The Top Step

Context: You are stocking a high shelf. You need just 2 more inches.

Decision: STOP. Never stand on the top step or the "This is not a step" rung.

Scenario B: Roof Access

Context: Ladder reaches the roof edge exactly. No overlap.

Decision: STOP. Ladder must extend 3 FEET past the landing surface to walk off safely.

6. Inspection

Check the feet. Are the rubber pads worn off? If so, you are climbing on metal skates.

Competency Check

Q1: If a ladder is 12ft high against a wall, how far out should the base be?

A: 3 Feet (12 divided by 4).

Q2: What is the "Belt Buckle" rule?

A: Keep your buckle between the rails to prevent overreaching.

Q3: Can you carry a tool box up a ladder in your hand?

A: No. You lose 3 points of contact. Use a rope or tool belt.
